How to Subscribe

The Hawaiʻi Monthly Climate Dashboard lets you explore rainfall, temperature, and drought conditions across the Hawaiian Islands by month, year, island, and geographic division.

Select a Month & Year

At the top of the dashboard, use the month and year dropdowns to pick the time period you want to explore. Use the ‹ › arrows to step one month at a time, or click Latest to jump straight to the most recent data. Data availability varies by dataset.

Choose a Dataset

Toggle between three datasets using the buttons in the Climate Snapshot panel:

Rainfall

Monthly precipitation totals and anomalies compared to the long-term average. Shows how wet or dry a period was relative to history.

Temperature

Average monthly temperature, anomaly from normal, and the highest recorded temperature for the period.

Drought

U.S. Drought Monitor categories derived from the 3-month Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI-3), which compares cumulative rainfall over the past three months to the historical distribution for that period.

Navigate the Map

The interactive map lets you zoom into specific areas. Start by choosing an Island, then refine further with a Division type:

Moku Traditional Hawaiian land districts — large regional sections of each island.
Ahupuaʻa Traditional land divisions running from mountain to sea — the finest geographic resolution available.
Climate NOAA climate divisions — the standard regions used for official long-term climate records.
Watershed Hydrological catchment areas — useful for understanding water resource patterns.

Click any region on the map to select it. The Climate Snapshot and chart panels will update to reflect conditions for that specific area. Click Reset to return to the statewide view.

Understanding the Statistics

Rainfall & Temperature Stats

Observed Average
The actual measured value for the selected month and area — inches of rain or degrees Fahrenheit. This is computed from station data within the selected region.
Anomaly
How much the observed value differs from the long-term average (normal) for the same month. A positive anomaly means wetter or warmer than normal; a negative anomaly means drier or cooler. The percentage shown (e.g. +23%) expresses the departure as a share of the normal value.
Rank
Where this month falls in the historical record — e.g. 2nd Wettest out of 30 years. Rank 1 is the most extreme (wettest, driest, warmest, or coolest depending on context).
Year-to-date % of Normal (Rainfall only)
Cumulative rainfall from January through the selected month as a percentage of the normal year-to-date total. Values above 100% mean above-normal accumulation so far this year.
Max Temp (Temperature only)
The highest average daily maximum temperature recorded in the region during the month.

Click any stat box to open the full ranked table for all years on record.

Drought Stats

Drought statistics show what percentage of the selected area falls into each U.S. Drought Monitor category, derived from the 3-month Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI-3). SPI-3 measures how unusual the past three months of rainfall are relative to the historical record — negative values indicate dryness, positive values indicate wetness. Categories are cumulative — D1 or worse includes all area in D1, D2, D3, and D4.

Step 5

Reading the Historical Chart

The chart on the right side of the dashboard shows the full historical record for your selected dataset, location, and month. Each bar or data point represents one year. The highlighted bar corresponds to the currently selected year.

  • Hover over bars to see exact values for individual years.
  • Use Expand to open the chart in a larger fullscreen view.
